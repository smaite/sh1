üéØ Key Principles of Great Instructions

Why is this important? Whether you're explaining how to bake cookies, build a robot, or navigate to a new location, clear instructions make the difference between success and confusion. Let's learn the essential principles!

Sequential Order

Present steps in the exact order they should be performed. Number each step clearly so readers don't get lost.

üíé

Clarity & Precision

Use simple, specific language. Avoid ambiguous words like "some" or "nearby." Say exactly what you mean.

Visual Language

Help readers picture each step. Use descriptive verbs and specific measurements or landmarks.

üé¨

Action Verbs

Start each step with a strong action verb: "Turn," "Mix," "Click," "Walk." This makes instructions direct and clear.

‚úÖ

Always test your instructions by following them yourself or having someone else try them.

Great instructions anticipate questions and confusion. Think about what might trip up your reader and address it proactively!

üîÑ Instructions vs. Directions: What's the Difference?

Know the difference! While both provide step-by-step guidance, instructions and directions serve different purposes. Understanding this helps you choose the right approach.

üìã Instructions

Purpose: Explain HOW to do or make something

Focus: Process, procedure, or task completion

Examples:

  • How to bake a cake
  • How to assemble furniture
  • How to use an app
  • How to perform CPR
  • How to write an essay

Key Elements:

  • Materials/tools needed
  • Preparation steps
  • Detailed procedures
  • Safety warnings
  • Expected results

üó∫Ô∏è Directions

Purpose: Explain HOW to get somewhere

Focus: Navigation from point A to point B

Examples:

  • How to get to the library
  • How to reach the park
  • How to find a classroom
  • How to navigate downtown
  • How to walk to a store

Key Elements:

  • Starting point
  • Landmarks to look for
  • Turns and distances
  • Final destination markers
  • Estimated travel time
Think of it this way: Instructions teach you to DO something; Directions teach you to GO somewhere!

How to Write Clear Instructions

Step 1: Choose a Clear Title ‚ñº

What to do: Start with "How to..." followed by the specific task.

Examples:

  • ‚úì "How to Make a Peanut Butter and Jelly Sandwich"
  • ‚úì "How to Create a Strong Password"
  • ‚úó "Sandwich Making" (too vague)
Step 2: List Materials/Tools Needed ‚ñº

What to do: Before the steps, create a complete list of everything needed.

Why: This lets readers gather everything first, making the process smoother.

Example:

Materials Needed:

  • 2 slices of bread
  • Peanut butter (creamy or chunky)
  • Jelly or jam
  • Butter knife
  • Plate
Step 3: Write Sequential Steps ‚ñº

What to do: Number each step and start with an action verb.

Tips:

  • Break complex steps into smaller sub-steps
  • Use consistent formatting
  • Include time estimates when relevant
  • Describe expected outcomes for key steps

Example:

1. Place two slices of bread on a clean plate.

2. Open the peanut butter jar and use the butter knife to scoop out about 2 tablespoons.

3. Spread the peanut butter evenly on one slice of bread, covering it from edge to edge.

Step 4: Add Warnings & Tips ‚ñº

What to do: Include helpful notes, warnings, or alternatives.

Types of notes:

  • ‚ö†Ô∏è Warning: "Do not use metal utensils on non-stick pans."
  • üí° Tip: "For extra flavor, toast the bread first."
  • ‚ÑπÔ∏è Note: "This step can take 5-10 minutes depending on your oven."
Step 5: End with Expected Results ‚ñº

What to do: Describe what success looks like.

Example: "Your sandwich should have peanut butter and jelly evenly distributed between two slices of bread. The sandwich can be cut diagonally or into rectangles for easier eating."

Instructions Writing Checklist ‚úì

Clear, descriptive title starting with "How to..."
Complete list of materials and tools
Steps numbered in chronological order
Each step begins with an action verb
Specific measurements and quantities included
Warnings placed BEFORE the relevant step
Tips and alternatives mentioned when helpful
Description of final result/expected outcome

How to Write Clear Directions

Step 1: Identify Start & End Points ‚ñº

What to do: Clearly state where the journey begins and ends.

Example:

Starting Point: School main entrance

Destination: City Public Library

Why: This orients your reader and sets expectations.

Step 2: Use Cardinal Directions & Landmarks ‚ñº

What to do: Combine compass directions (north, south, east, west) with visible landmarks.

Good examples:

  • ‚úì "Head north on Main Street toward the tall red-brick church"
  • ‚úì "Turn right at the McDonald's with the playground"
  • ‚úì "Walk past the fountain in the center of the park"

Avoid:

  • ‚úó "Go that way for a while" (too vague)
  • ‚úó "Turn at the third tree" (not distinctive enough)
Step 3: Include Distances & Time ‚ñº

What to do: Give readers a sense of how far and how long.

Examples:

  • "Walk straight for about 0.3 miles (5 minutes)"
  • "Continue for two blocks until you reach the intersection"
  • "Drive approximately 10 minutes on Highway 101"

Tip: Estimate time for the mode of travel (walking vs. driving).

Step 4: Write Step-by-Step Directions ‚ñº

What to do: Number each direction change or significant point.

Action verbs for directions:

  • Turn (left/right)
  • Continue straight
  • Cross (the street/intersection)
  • Pass (a landmark)
  • Head (toward)
  • Proceed (along)
Step 5: Confirm Arrival ‚ñº

What to do: Describe what the destination looks like so readers know they've arrived.

Example: "The library is a modern glass building on your right with blue banners hanging from the entrance. The main doors are at the front, and there's a bike rack to the left."

Directions Writing Checklist ‚úì

Clear starting point mentioned
Destination clearly identified
Specific landmarks used for reference
Cardinal directions (N, S, E, W) included when possible
Distances or time estimates provided
Each turn or change numbered sequentially
Action verbs used (turn, walk, cross, etc.)
Destination appearance described for confirmation

Example 1: Making Hot Chocolate

‚ùå BAD EXAMPLE

How to Make Hot Chocolate

Get some milk and chocolate. Heat it up until it's hot. Add the chocolate and stir it. Pour it in a cup and drink it.

Why it's bad:

  • No materials list
  • Vague measurements ("some milk")
  • Unclear instructions ("heat it up")
  • Steps are out of order
  • No specific temperatures or times
‚úÖ GOOD EXAMPLE

How to Make Hot Chocolate

Materials Needed:

  • 1 cup of milk (whole or 2%)
  • 2 tablespoons cocoa powder
  • 1-2 tablespoons sugar
  • Small saucepan
  • Whisk or spoon
  • Mug

Instructions:

1 Pour 1 cup of milk into a small saucepan.

2 Add 2 tablespoons of cocoa powder and 1-2 tablespoons of sugar to the milk.

3 Turn the stove to medium heat and place the saucepan on the burner.

4 Stir constantly with a whisk for 3-5 minutes until the mixture is hot and steaming (do not let it boil).

5 Remove the saucepan from heat once you see small bubbles forming at the edges.

6 Carefully pour the hot chocolate into your mug.

‚ö†Ô∏è Warning: The saucepan and liquid will be very hot. Use an oven mitt when handling.

üí° Tip: Top with marshmallows or whipped cream for extra deliciousness!

Result: You should have a smooth, chocolatey drink that's hot but not boiling, with no powder lumps.

Example 2: Taking a Screenshot

‚ùå BAD EXAMPLE

How to Screenshot

Press some buttons on your keyboard and it saves somewhere on your computer.

Why it's bad:

  • Doesn't specify which device (Windows, Mac, phone)
  • "Some buttons" is completely unclear
  • No information about where to find the screenshot
  • Missing step-by-step instructions
‚úÖ GOOD EXAMPLE

How to Take a Screenshot on Windows

Method 1: Full Screen Screenshot

1 Make sure the screen shows exactly what you want to capture.

2 Press the Windows key + Print Screen buttons at the same time.

3 Your screen will briefly dim, indicating the screenshot was taken.

4 Open File Explorer and navigate to This PC > Pictures > Screenshots.

5 Your screenshot will be saved here with the date and time as the filename.

Method 2: Partial Screen Screenshot

1 Press Windows key + Shift + S.

2 Your screen will dim and a cursor will appear.

3 Click and drag to select the area you want to capture.

4 Release the mouse button when the correct area is highlighted.

5 The screenshot is copied to your clipboard. Open any program (like Paint or Word) and press Ctrl + V to paste it.

üí° Tip: Use Method 1 for quick full-screen captures, Method 2 when you only need part of the screen.

Example 3: Walking to the Park

‚ùå BAD EXAMPLE

Directions to the Park

Go down the street and turn. Keep walking until you see trees. The park is over there.

Why it's bad:

  • No starting point specified
  • "Turn" - which direction? Left or right?
  • "Keep walking" - for how long?
  • No specific landmarks
  • "Over there" is meaningless in written directions
‚úÖ GOOD EXAMPLE

Walking Directions from Lincoln High School to Riverside Park

Starting Point: Main entrance of Lincoln High School

Total Distance: 0.7 miles (approximately 15 minutes walking)

1 Exit through the main doors and turn right onto Oak Street heading east.

2 Walk straight on Oak Street for three blocks (about 5 minutes). You'll pass a Starbucks on your left and a CVS pharmacy on your right.

3 At the third intersection, you'll reach Maple Avenue. Turn left (north) at the traffic light with the crosswalk.

4 Continue north on Maple Avenue for two blocks (about 4 minutes). You'll walk past an elementary school with a yellow playground on your right.

5 Cross River Road at the stoplight. Use the pedestrian crossing button and wait for the walk signal.

6 After crossing, you'll see the park entrance directly ahead. Look for the stone pillars with "Riverside Park" engraved on them and a large oak tree between them.

7 Walk through the stone pillars. The main picnic area is 100 feet straight ahead, and the playground is to your left.

‚ö†Ô∏è Safety Note: Cross all streets at designated crosswalks and obey traffic signals.

üí° Tip: If you reach the gas station on the corner of Maple and Pine, you've gone one block too far‚Äîturn back!

Example 4: Driving to the Airport

‚ùå BAD EXAMPLE

Directions to Airport

Take the highway north and follow signs. Get off at the airport exit. You'll see planes.

Why it's bad:

  • Which highway? There could be multiple.
  • No specific exit number
  • No distance or time estimates
  • Doesn't specify which terminal or parking area
  • No information about lanes or merging
‚úÖ GOOD EXAMPLE

Driving Directions to Regional Airport - Terminal B Departures

Starting Point: Downtown Convention Center parking garage

Total Distance: 12.5 miles (approximately 20 minutes without traffic)

1 Exit the parking garage and turn right onto Convention Street.

2 Drive 0.3 miles and merge onto Highway 95 North using the on-ramp on your right.

3 Stay in the right two lanes after merging. Continue on Highway 95 North for 8.5 miles (about 12 minutes).

4 Watch for green airport signs. Take Exit 47B marked "Regional Airport - All Terminals."

5 After exiting, stay in the left lane and follow signs for "Terminal B - Departures."

6 At the fork, bear left toward Terminal B. The right fork goes to Terminal A.

7 Follow the elevated roadway for 0.5 miles. You'll see the terminal building on your left.

8 Follow signs for either "Hourly Parking" (garage on left) or "Short-Term Parking" (right side) depending on your needs.

‚ö†Ô∏è Important: Terminal B has a departure drop-off zone on Level 2. If dropping off passengers, follow signs for "Departures Upper Level."

üí° Tip: During rush hour (7-9 AM and 4-6 PM), add 15-20 minutes to your travel time.

Destination: Terminal B is a modern white building with large windows. The entrance has automatic sliding doors and electronic flight information displays above them.

Notice how good examples always include: specific numbers, clear landmarks, action verbs, and descriptions of what to look for. Practice writing your own using these as models!

Common Mistakes to Avoid

‚ùå Don't Do This

  • Use vague words: "some," "a little," "nearby," "soon"
  • Skip steps that seem obvious to you
  • Assume everyone knows technical terms
  • Write paragraphs instead of numbered steps
  • Forget to mention materials needed
  • Put warnings after the dangerous step
  • Use passive voice: "The button should be pressed"

‚úÖ Do This Instead

  • Use specific words: "2 cups," "200 feet," "at the corner," "for 5 minutes"
  • Include every single step, even simple ones
  • Define terms or use simpler language
  • Number each step clearly
  • List materials at the beginning
  • Place warnings before the relevant step
  • Use active voice: "Press the button"
The ultimate test: Can someone who has never done this task successfully complete it using only your instructions? That's the gold standard!
