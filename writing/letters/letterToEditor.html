Writing Letters to the Editor

By The Editorial Board
"A well-written letter to the editor is a powerful tool for civic engagement and public discourse."

What is a Letter to the Editor?

A letter to the editor is a formal letter written to a newspaper or magazine expressing opinions, concerns, or responses to published articles. It allows citizens to participate in public dialogue and influence community thinking.

Purpose & Importance

These letters serve multiple purposes: expressing viewpoints on current issues, correcting misinformation, praising good work, suggesting solutions to problems, or responding to other readers' letters. For SEE exam purpose, they are typically around 200 words—concise yet persuasive.

Essential Guidelines

  • Be concise: Stay within the 200 word limit
  • Focus on one issue: Address a single topic clearly
  • Reference appropriately: Mention the article you're responding to
  • Provide evidence: Support your arguments with facts
  • Maintain respectful tone: Even when disagreeing
  • Include contact information: Most publications verify authorship
  • Proofread carefully: Errors reduce credibility

Letter Format Guide

  1. Sender's Address: Your complete address
  2. Date: Date of writing the letter
  3. Editor's Address: Newspaper's editorial office
  4. Salutation: "Dear Editor" or "Sir/Madam"
  5. Subject Line: Clear reference to topic
  6. Introduction: State purpose and reference
  7. Body: Present arguments logically
  8. Conclusion: Summarize and suggest action
  9. Closing: "Yours sincerely/faithfully"
  10. Signature & Name: With contact details
Pro Tip: Always check the newspaper's specific submission guidelines for word limits and formatting requirements.

What to DO

  • Start with a polite tone and state your position in the first paragraph
  • Use facts and logical arguments
  • Keep paragraphs short and focused
  • Suggest constructive solutions
  • Proofread for clarity and correctness
  • Include your contact information

What NOT to Do

  • Don't exceed the word limit
  • Avoid personal attacks or insults
  • Don't make unsubstantiated claims
  • Avoid overly emotional language
  • Don't include irrelevant information
  • Avoid complicated jargon
  • Don't forget to include your name
