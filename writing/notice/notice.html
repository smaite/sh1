üìã What is a Notice?

A notice is a formal written announcement that provides important information to a specific group of people. It's designed to be displayed publicly (on bulletin boards, walls, websites) to inform people about events, meetings, changes, or important updates. Think of it as an official memo that everyone needs to see!
üéØ

Purpose

To inform, announce, or instruct a large group of people about something important

üë•

Audience

General public or specific groups like students, employees, club members, residents

üìè

Length

Short and concise‚Äîtypically 50-100 words. No unnecessary details!

üíº

Tone

Formal, direct, and professional. No casual language or emotions

üì¶

Format

Follows a strict structure with a box/border. Always organized and neat

‚è∞

Timeliness

Posted well in advance with clear dates, times, and deadlines

Remember the 5 W's: A good notice answers Who, What, When, Where, and Why (if necessary). Keep it clear, keep it brief, and make every word count!

üèóÔ∏è The Perfect Notice Format

Format is everything! A notice must follow a specific structure. Missing even one element can cost you marks. Let's break down each component:
Part 1

üìç Organization/Institution Name

Position: Top center, in capital letters

Example: GREENWOOD HIGH SCHOOL

Purpose: Identifies who is issuing the notice

Part 2

üì¢ The Word "NOTICE"

Position: Center, below the organization name, underlined

Format: All capitals, underlined

Purpose: Immediately identifies the document type

Part 3

üìÖ Date

Position: Left side, below "NOTICE"

Format: Day Month, Year (e.g., 15 March, 2024)

Purpose: Shows when the notice was issued

Part 4

üí≠ Subject/Heading

Position: Center or left, brief and specific

Example: Subject: Annual Sports Day Postponement

Purpose: Summarizes the main topic in one line

Part 5

üìù Body

Content: The main message with all essential details

Structure: 3-5 sentences covering Who, What, When, Where, Why/How

Style: Third person, passive voice, formal language

Part 6

‚úçÔ∏è Signature

Position: Right side at the bottom

Content: Name and designation of the issuer

Example: John Smith
Sports Secretary

Pro Tip: Always enclose your notice in a box or border! This is a non-negotiable requirement that makes it look professional and official.

‚öñÔ∏è Do's and Don'ts of Notice Writing

Do's

  • ‚úì Use a box or border around the entire notice
  • ‚úì Keep it brief‚Äî50 to 100 words maximum
  • ‚úì Use formal, official language
  • ‚úì Write in third person (avoid "I" or "We")
  • ‚úì Include all essential details (date, time, venue)
  • ‚úì Put the most important information first
  • ‚úì Use passive voice where appropriate
  • ‚úì Proofread for grammar and spelling
  • ‚úì Include contact information if needed
  • ‚úì Make headings bold or underlined

Don'ts

  • ‚úó Don't write in outside your border line.
  • ‚úó Don't use casual or emotional language
  • ‚úó Don't exceed the word limit
  • ‚úó Don't forget to sign with name and designation
  • ‚úó Don't use abbreviations (write "Physical Education" not "PE")
  • ‚úó Don't include unnecessary details
  • ‚úó Don't forget to mention the date and time
  • ‚úó Don't use decorative fonts or colors
  • ‚úó Don't write complete sentences when points work better
  • ‚úó Don't forget the box/border!

üéØ Quick Reference: Notice Writing Steps
